# Form Mapping

The prototype uses normalized page coordinates in `src/form_mapping.py` so regions scale with page size. These regions are tuned around TTB F 5100.31 page one and are intended to be easy to adjust. Scanned application image files are converted to an in-memory PDF page first, then the same normalized regions are applied.

Sources reviewed: the TTB Forms page lists current TTB F 5100.31 as `Application for and Certification/Exemption of Label/Bottle Approval (04/2023)`, and the linked form PDF includes the page-one fields and instructions used for this mapping. TTB's formula approval basics guidance states that the Formula ID is needed before applying for label approval and that final alcohol content is part of the formula application information. TTB's distilled spirits, wine, and malt beverage example formula pages show Formulas Online entries with a Yield Summary section and an `Alcohol Content of Finished Product` row. eCFR 27 CFR 4.21 identifies wine standards of identity including dessert wine types such as sherry and port, sparkling wine types such as champagne, sake as wine from other agricultural products, and vermouth as aperitif wine. eCFR 27 CFR Part 24 identifies hard cider as wine for the hard-cider tax rate and describes cider/perry materials under the wine rules.

The current TTB F 5100.31 instructions place brand, fanciful name, product type, formula/pre-COLA reference, wine-only grape varietals, wine appellation, contact details, application type, and Item 15 container text in page-one form fields. TTB's formula approval basics guidance describes formulas as recipes with detailed ingredients and manufacturing steps, and identifies final alcohol content as formula application information. TTB's example formulas show that the final-product alcohol content may be a low/high range in the Formulas Online Yield Summary. In this prototype, Formula ID lookup is local to the uploaded PDF package and requires an exact normalized ID match: the app does not call Formulas Online or any remote TTB service.

The blank TTB F 5100.31 form does not always expose class/type, alcohol content, net contents, bottler/producer, or country of origin as simple fields. Item 3 source-of-product and Item 5 product-type checkbox widgets are read directly when the PDF preserves widgets; flattened source-form pages use visible checkbox-mark detection against the current form layout. Multiple selected checkboxes for one of these mutually exclusive groups are treated as ambiguous source data and routed to Needs Review. For expected values not present as first-page fields, the extractor also looks for an explicit application-data summary block inside the completed PDF. That block is treated as application/package metadata, not label evidence.

Alcohol-content checks normalize percent ABV, proof, expected proof ranges such as `80-90 Proof` and `80 Proof - 90 Proof`, compact `vol` statements, repeated-unit ranges such as `12.5%-13.5% ABV`, decimal-comma ABV statements such as `13,5% vol`, and decimal-comma expected ranges such as `12,5-13,5% ABV`. Net contents checks normalize milliliters, liters, centiliters, fluid ounces, plain ounces used as net contents, dotted or OCR-split metric units such as `750 M.L.` and `750 M L`, compact estimated quantity marks such as `e750 mL`, comma thousands such as `1,000 mL`, whole or fractional pints, spelled-out number words such as `Seven Hundred Fifty Milliliters`, rounded dual-unit statements such as `750 mL / 25.4 FL OZ`, and compound pint/ounce statements, and ignore serving-size, recipe, calorie, and per-serving volumes so unrelated label text does not create a false mismatch. Product-type checks treat descriptor-only wine wording such as `Wine Barrel Finished` as non-conflicting when a clear distilled-spirits class/type statement is present, but explicit product-type statements such as `WINE` still control and conflicting explicit product-type statements are routed to Needs Review. Wine-category product-type checks recognize hard cider, perry, and 27 CFR 4.21 wine class/type terms that may appear without the literal word `Wine`, including `Sake`, `Vermouth`, `Sherry`, `Port`, and `Champagne`. Class/type checks use explicit `Class/Type` values first and route conflicting explicit class/type statements to Needs Review. Brand and responsible-party matching normalize harmless legal suffix variants such as `Co.`/`Company`, `Inc.`/`Incorporated`, `Corp.`/`Corporation`, `Ltd.`/`Limited`, and `L.L.C.`/`LLC`, and recognize comma, slash, ampersand, and `and` separators in action lists such as `Distilled, bottled and packaged by` or `Produced/Bottled by`, while different explicit suffixes are not treated as the same entity; same-role responsible-party conflicts such as two different `Bottled by` entities are routed to Needs Review, while separate roles such as `Bottled by` and `Imported by` are not treated as conflicts by themselves. Imported country checks require origin-style label wording, such as `Product of Mexico`, `Produced in Mexico`, `Distilled in Mexico`, `Distilled and bottled in Scotland`, `Distilled, matured and bottled in Scotland`, `Blended in Scotland`, `Brewed in Belgium`, `Wine of France`, `Made in Mexico`, `Imported from Mexico`, `Imported by ... from Mexico`, or `Country of Origin Mexico`; a country name appearing only in an importer company name is not enough for a Pass, and conflicting origin-style country statements are routed to Needs Review. `United States` may also match common origin abbreviations such as `USA`, `U.S.A.`, and `U.S.`; `United Kingdom` may match `UK`, `U.K.`, `Great Britain`, protected `Scotch Whisky`, or constituent-origin wording such as `Product of Scotland`, `Whisky of Scotland`, `Distilled in Scotland`, `Distilled and bottled in Scotland`, `Distilled, matured and bottled in Scotland`, or `Blended in Scotland`; `Ireland` may match `Republic of Ireland` or `Irish Whiskey`; `Canada` may match `Canadian Whisky`; and `Netherlands` may match `The Netherlands` when these variants appear in origin-style wording.

When `docs/source/f510031.pdf` is available locally, the sample generator fills the real TTB form template and adds an invisible application-data summary text layer for expected values that are not first-page fields. If the source form is absent, it falls back to a controlled TTB-like one-page layout.
