# Form Mapping

The prototype uses normalized page coordinates in `src/form_mapping.py` so regions scale with page size. These regions are tuned around TTB F 5100.31 page one and are intended to be easy to adjust. Scanned application image files are converted to an in-memory PDF page first, then the same normalized regions are applied.

Sources reviewed: the TTB Forms page lists current TTB F 5100.31 as `Application for and Certification/Exemption of Label/Bottle Approval (04/2023)`, and the linked form PDF includes the page-one fields and instructions used for this mapping. TTB's formula approval basics guidance states that the Formula ID is needed before applying for label approval and that final alcohol content is part of the formula application information. TTB's distilled spirits, wine, and malt beverage example formula pages show Formulas Online entries with a Yield Summary section and an `Alcohol Content of Finished Product` row. eCFR 27 CFR 5.66 lists distilled-spirits function statements such as `bottled by`, `filled by`, `made by`, `prepared by`, `produced by`, `manufactured by`, and `distilled by`; eCFR 27 CFR 4.35 treats wine `distributed by` text as an additional statement rather than a replacement for required bottler/packer/importer evidence. eCFR 27 CFR 4.21 identifies wine standards of identity including dessert wine types such as sherry and port, sparkling wine types such as champagne, sake as wine from other agricultural products, and vermouth as aperitif wine. eCFR 27 CFR 4.23 states that grape variety names may be used as type designations for grape wine when the regulatory conditions are met. eCFR 27 CFR Part 24 identifies hard cider as wine for the hard-cider tax rate and describes cider/perry materials under the wine rules.

The current TTB F 5100.31 instructions place brand, fanciful name, product type, formula/pre-COLA reference, wine-only grape varietals, wine appellation, contact details, application type, and Item 15 container text in page-one form fields. TTB's formula approval basics guidance describes formulas as recipes with detailed ingredients and manufacturing steps, and identifies final alcohol content as formula application information. TTB's example formulas show that the final-product alcohol content may be a low/high range in the Formulas Online Yield Summary. In this prototype, Formula ID lookup is local to the uploaded PDF package and requires an exact normalized ID match: the app does not call Formulas Online or any remote TTB service.

The blank TTB F 5100.31 form does not always expose class/type, alcohol content, net contents, bottler/producer, or country of origin as simple fields. Item 3 source-of-product and Item 5 product-type checkbox widgets are read directly when the PDF preserves widgets; flattened source-form pages use visible checkbox-mark detection against the current form layout. Multiple selected checkboxes for one of these mutually exclusive groups are treated as ambiguous source data and routed to Needs Review. For expected values not present as first-page fields, the extractor also looks for an explicit application-data summary block inside the completed PDF. That block is treated as application/package metadata, not label evidence.

Alcohol-content checks normalize percent ABV, proof, adjacent dual proof/ABV statements such as `90 Proof 45% Alc./Vol.`, expected proof ranges such as `80-90 Proof` and `80 Proof - 90 Proof`, compact `vol` statements such as `45% Alcohol Vol.` or `45% Alcohol/Vol.`, repeated-unit ranges such as `12.5%-13.5% ABV`, decimal-comma ABV statements such as `13,5% vol`, and decimal-comma expected ranges such as `12,5-13,5% ABV`. Non-alcohol percentages such as `100% Agave`, ingredient percent-by-volume statements such as `Natural flavoring 49% by volume`, and non-ABV volume text such as `Alcohol Volume 50 mL` are ignored so they do not create false ABV mismatches. Net contents checks normalize milliliters, liters, centiliters, fluid ounces, plain ounces used as net contents, dotted or OCR-split metric units such as `750 M.L.` and `750 M L`, compact estimated quantity marks such as `e750 mL`, comma thousands such as `1,000 mL`, whole or fractional pints, spelled-out number words such as `Seven Hundred Fifty Milliliters`, rounded dual-unit statements such as `750 mL / 25.4 FL OZ`, compound pint/ounce statements, and multipack statements such as `4 x 50 mL`, `4-50 mL`, `4-pack of 12 fl oz`, or `4/12 fl oz cans`, and ignore serving-size, recipe, calorie, and per-serving volumes so unrelated label text does not create a false mismatch. Product-type checks treat descriptor-only wine wording such as `Wine Barrel Finished` as non-conflicting when a clear distilled-spirits class/type statement is present, and distilled-spirits specialty/cocktail class-type designations can satisfy Item 5 even without a separate `DISTILLED SPIRITS` product-type line; this includes first-line `Class/Type` evidence such as `Class/Type: Liqueur`. Explicit product-type statements such as `WINE` still control and conflicting explicit product-type statements are routed to Needs Review. Wine-category product-type checks recognize hard cider, perry, 27 CFR 4.21 wine class/type terms that may appear without the literal word `Wine`, including `Sake`, `Vermouth`, `Sherry`, `Port`, and `Champagne`, and common 27 CFR 4.23 grape-varietal type designations such as `Chardonnay`, `Cabernet Sauvignon`, `Pinot Noir`, `Merlot`, and `Riesling`. Supplied Item 10 grape-varietal values and Item 11 wine-appellation values are checked separately from product type; missing label evidence for those supplied wine-only values becomes Needs Review. Class/type checks use explicit `Class/Type` values first, preserve values that include product-type words such as `Distilled Spirits Specialty`, and route conflicting explicit class/type statements to Needs Review. Brand and responsible-party matching normalize harmless legal suffix variants such as `Co.`/`Company`, `Inc.`/`Incorporated`, `Corp.`/`Corporation`, `Ltd.`/`Limited`, and `L.L.C.`/`LLC`, and recognize colon punctuation after `by` or `for`, comma, slash, ampersand, and `and` separators in action lists such as `Distilled, bottled and packaged by`, `Produced/Bottled by`, or `Imported, bottled and distributed by` plus limited modifiers before `by` or `for`, such as `Bottled exclusively for`, `Produced specially by`, and `Imported solely by`, while different explicit suffixes are not treated as the same entity; same-role responsible-party conflicts such as two different `Bottled by` entities are routed to Needs Review, while separate roles such as `Bottled by` and `Imported by` are not treated as conflicts by themselves. `Filled by`, `Made by`, `Prepared by`, `Manufactured by`, `Mfg. by`, and `Mfr. by` are recognized as function statements, but `Distributed by` alone is not treated as sufficient bottler/producer evidence and is routed to Needs Review. Brand text that only matches after OCR-like character substitutions such as `0` for `O` is routed to Needs Review rather than Pass or a confident material failure. Imported country checks require origin-style label wording, such as `Product of Mexico`, `Produced in Mexico`, `Produced, bottled and packaged in France`, `Distilled in Mexico`, `Distilled and bottled in Scotland`, `Distilled, matured and bottled in Scotland`, `Blended in Scotland`, `Brewed in Belgium`, `Brewed and canned in Belgium`, `Wine of France`, `Made in Mexico`, `Made and bottled in Italy`, `Imported from Mexico`, `Imported by ... from Mexico`, or `Country of Origin Mexico`; a country name appearing only in an importer company name or bottling-location-only wording such as `Bottled in Mexico` is not enough for a Pass, and conflicting origin-style country statements are routed to Needs Review. Domestic/no-country-required applications pass when no origin is claimed, but non-U.S. origin-style label wording such as `Product of Mexico` is routed to Needs Review because it contradicts the domestic source context. `United States` may also match common origin abbreviations such as `USA`, `U.S.A.`, and `U.S.`; `United Kingdom` may match `UK`, `U.K.`, `Great Britain`, protected `Scotch Whisky`, or constituent-origin wording such as `Product of Scotland`, `Whisky of Scotland`, `Distilled in Scotland`, `Distilled and bottled in Scotland`, `Distilled, matured and bottled in Scotland`, or `Blended in Scotland`; `Ireland` may match `Republic of Ireland` or `Irish Whiskey`; `Canada` may match `Canadian Whisky`; and `Netherlands` may match `The Netherlands` when these variants appear in origin-style wording.

When `docs/source/f510031.pdf` is available locally, the sample generator fills the real TTB form template and adds an invisible application-data summary text layer for expected values that are not first-page fields. If the source form is absent, it falls back to a controlled TTB-like one-page layout.
